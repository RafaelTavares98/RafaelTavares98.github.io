Case description

A food company wants to produce the highest profit for the next direct marketing campaign, scheduled for the next month.

A pilot campaign involving 2.240 customers was carried out, resulting in a 15% success rate (15% or 336 people said "Yes" and bought the offer while the other 85% or 1904 didn't).

The campaign had a total cost of 6.720 MU and resulted in a profit of -3.406 MU. Resulting in:

  • Cost per Contact (CPC) of 3.0 MU
  • Cost per Acquisition (CPA) of 20.0 MU
  • Revenue per Sale (Average) of 10.9 MU

The Problem: On average, it costs the company 20 MU to "convince" someone to buy. But that person only spends 10.93 MU on average, therefore, the company is losing 9.07 per sale.

In order to improve this scenario, customer data was analyzed to develop a Tableau dashboard to better describe customer behavior.

To do that, we must cover 3 main objectives:

  • Which customers are the most profitable to target?
  • Which products are the most profitable to target?
  • How does the investment relate to profitability?

Ifood Data Insights

The visual analysis of the campaign data provides direct answers to the core objectives, outlining a clear path to profitability:

1. Which customers are the most profitable to target?

The optimal target audience earns between 60k and 99k annually and holds a higher education degree (Graduation, Master, or PhD). Customers earning below 60k consistently result in a negative ROI and should be excluded from future campaigns.

2. Which products are the most profitable to target?

Wines and Meat Products are the primary profit drivers, aligning perfectly with the 60k-99k income bracket. Low-margin categories (Fruits, Sweets, Fish) generate negligible revenue and operate at a net loss.

3. How does the investment relate to profitability?

Maximum profitability is achieved at a single point of contact (3 MU). Repeatedly contacting the same customer yields strictly diminishing returns. Investments surpassing 15 MU per customer result in a total collapse of ROI.
